This Was Never Just "News"

Reflecting on the BBC’s ‘Shot Like Enemies’ and the landslide that changed Nepal.

Over the past few weeks, something interesting has been happening in Nepal. A BBC documentary didn’t just trend—it triggered arguments at tea shops, heated Facebook threads, and serious political discussions.

"For some, it felt validating. For others, uncomfortable. For many, it felt personal."

The Emotional Pulse

For young people in Nepal, the protests weren’t a headline. They were emotions. They were frustration about jobs, anger about corruption, and questions about freedom—especially in the digital space.

When the BBC released "Shot Like Enemies", it reopened wounds that never fully healed. Some viewers said they felt seen; others felt it was unnecessary drama before the March 5th elections.

Why They Appreciated It

  • Accountability: Asking the hard questions local media moved on from.
  • Visibility: Bringing international eyes to youth political sidelining.
  • Voice: Giving space to those who felt unheard.

Why They Felt Uncomfortable

  • Timing: Fears of influencing the House of Representatives vote.
  • Narrative: Concerns about simplifying complex structural issues.
  • Stability: The belief that foreign media should be more cautious.

The Real Issue Isn't the BBC

This controversy is about who controls the narrative and whether young voices are taken seriously. The Election Commission’s request to remove the content only highlighted how fragile political trust remains.

The landslide victory of the Rastriya Swatantra Party proves one thing: Nepal’s democracy is messy, emotional, and loud—but it is very much alive.
